# Position

This pillar defines the market landscape, the category it occupies, how it's positioned relative to alternatives. Every competitive contrast, every differentiator, every "why us" claim should trace back to this pillar.

## Messaging Blocks

### Market Landscape

[Instructions:
Provide an opinionated description of the market space you operate in. Focus on scenarios, trends, inflection points, and constraints shaping buyer behavior. This should explain why the market looks the way it does today and why change is necessary.]

[Tips:
- Anchor on market forces — technology shifts, operating models, regulatory changes, economic impacts, competitive moves
- Speak to industry trends — big movements, hype cycles, converging/consolidating/dying categories
- This is the factual read on the market, not your pitch — your perspective on what it means belongs in Positioning]

[Format:
2-3 short paragraphs describing the market]

### Market Trends

[Instructions:
Document the industry-wide forces, movements, and directions that affect the company's position. These should be real, verifiable trends — not aspirational claims. For each trend, note whether it favors or challenges your position.]

[Tips:
- Include both tailwinds (trends that strengthen your positioning) and headwinds (trends that pressure it)
- A trend you ignore is a trend a competitor will use against you — name the headwinds honestly
- Connect trends to specific messaging implications — a trend without a messaging consequence is trivia]

[Format:
For each trend:
- **Trend:** [what's happening]
- **Direction:** [tailwind | headwind | neutral]
- **Messaging implication:** [how this affects positioning, differentiation, or urgency]]

### Positioning Statement

[Instructions:
State who you're for, why you're different, and what the buyer gains. A positioning statement isn't a tagline or a pitch — it's the internal anchor that every piece of external messaging derives from. It captures the intersection of your target audience, their unmet need, your category, and your unique advantage.

The framework below ensures completeness. Fill in each component, then read the full statement end-to-end — it should flow as a single coherent argument for why you exist.]

[Tips:
- Read April Dunford's Obviously Awesome for the thinking behind this framework
- Aim for your vision, but stay closer to reality — positioning that outruns proof erodes trust
- "Unlike" should reference the category of alternative, not a specific competitor by name
- "Only Us" is the hardest line to fill — if you can't articulate it, your differentiation isn't clear enough yet]

[Format:
- **For** (target audience)
- **Who** (statement of need)
- **And want** (statement of expectations)
- **We are** (primary position / category)
- **That** (statement of key benefit)
- **Unlike** (alternatives)
- **We** (primary differentiation)
- **Only us** (unique approach)
- **With us** (primary gain)]
